David Lawrence Miller

Biography

I am a postdoctoral fellow at the Research Unit for Wildlife Population Assessment (RUWPA) at the University of St Andrews where I work on building software and methods for calculating the abundances of biological populations (put simply: I count animals, using statistics and computers). My main interests are models for detection (accounting for how observers might miss seeing animals, usually involving their distance) and building spatial models (where are the animals, and how do they relate to biological factors like vegetation cover and sea depth?).

Before that (2012-2013) I was a postdoc at the Department of Natural Resources Science at the University of Rhode Island. There I worked on developing spatial distribution maps of seabirds off the coast of Rhode Island as well as developing statistical methods for spatial data. I worked with Scott McWilliams, Peter Paton and Kris Winiarski.

Previous to that (2011-2012) I was a postdoctoral research fellow working at the Centre for Research into Ecological and Environmental Modelling at the University of St Andrews.

From 2008-2011(12) I undertook a PhD at the University of Bath where I was investigating smoothing over complex regions under the supervision of Simon Wood. You can download (and even read) my thesis should that interest you. We published a paper in Environmental and Ecological Statistics with some of these results, which is significantly shorter.

I have a first class (MMath) degree in statistics from the University of St Andrews, where I spent my summers eating cake working at CREEM.

I live and work in New York, NY (but I'm enjoying travelling around the USA; code). I spend my spare time goofing around with computers, cooking, playing board games, visitng microbreweries, playing Kubb, building electronic things, running and taking photos. You can look at the things I find interesting on the internet on my tumblog.

Software

I write software. A lot of the software involves the package Distance, a Windows program for analysing distance sampling data. I'm part of a team of about 5 people around the world who develop the software. My work is mainly on the R components.

Most of my ongoing projects can be found on my github profile. Here is a (rough) list of software I've developed or helped develop.

Distance An R package (not to be confused with the Windows program) that allows one to perform simple distance sampling analyses. (Also available on CRAN.) This is basically just a more user friendly version of mrds...

dsm An R package for spatial modelling of distance sampling data (following the approach of Buckland and Hedley, 2004). As part of my (first) postdoc at CREEM, I re-wrote most of the previous implementation (by Eric Rexstad and Louise Burt) and included new features (see poster and paper below). If you're having issues, then look at the dsm wiki, where I am attempting to collate common questions.

mrds An R package that allows you to perform more complex analyses of distance sampling data (including things like double observer studies, to account for imperfect detection on the transect line). Jeff Laake was the package maintainer until 2014 and I have now taken over, having contributed code since starting on the team in 2005. (mrds is available on CRAN.)

mmds An R package for performing distance analyses using mixture model detection functions. This formulation allows you to avoid non-monotonically decreasing detction functions, which usually cause bias in analyses. (The package can be found on CRAN too.)

msg A way of performing smoothing (using splines in a generalized additive model framework) over a geographical region with a complex shape (for example peninsulae or coastlines). The github page has an example of how to use msg. This is an implementation of the methods I developed as part of my PhD.
